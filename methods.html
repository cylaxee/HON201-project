Methods and Design

Methods:

I would conduct a participant survey where participants will answer a number of questions about what they regularly eat in a week, how many credits they are currently taking, if they are working (if so, the number of hours weekly), and what they consider to be a healthy diet.

I chose this method because I think that I am not going to be extracting a great deal of information from any one individual; I think that the results of the research would best be represented with a large sample. Surveys are great for asking a few questions from a lot of people.

The people that would be tested would be undergraduate students at Portland State. This group is around 20-40 years old and entirely within the Portland Area. I do not think it is important to distinguish between students who live on campus or students who live off campus in apartments or with their parents. I selected this group of people because of its accessibility to me; I am an undergraduate student at Portland State that regularly attends classes on campus. The added benefit of conducting a survey would be that I would not have to physically meet with any participants; I could conceivably post a QR code that links to an online survey on a few places across campus.
I do not believe that anybody should be excluded among this group that I specified.

Design:

The variation (the independent variable) would arise in considering how “busy” each participant is; Perhaps the participant works many hours per week or has a heavy course load, or perhaps the participant is not a full-time student or does not work.

Stimulus sentences that I would present to participants would be the questions:

How many credits are you currently taking?
If you have a job, how many hours per week do you usually work?
Do you consider yourself to have a lot of free time?
How often do you cook for yourself in a week?
What do they usually eat in a week (maybe how many meals do they typically eat per day)?
What do you consider to be a healthy diet, and do you consider yourself to have one?

To confirm my hypothesis, participants who consider themselves to have a healthy diet must more often have a lighter schedule than a heavier schedule(considering both school and work).
To disconfirm my hypothesis, there would not be a significant difference between heavy and light schedule groups in regards to whether or not they consider themselves to have a healthy diet.

Procedure:

Participants will scan a QR code with their phone camera to access an online survey; on the survey, the questions on the survey would be the same as the stimulus sentences; in addition, there would be a question for extra comments that the participant may want to add.
All the participants would need to do is answer the survey questions truthfully.
Since this procedure will occur entirely outside of my proctoring, the step-by-step protocol would vary slightly; but, participants will begin the survey after scanning a QR code that links to the survey.

Analysis:

The questions in the survey are fairly specific enough that I believe that it would be possible to display the results on a sort of bar graph and a pie chart; The bar graph would compare individuals based on the questions regarding their schedule; the pie chart would visualize what percent of people considered themselves to have a healthy diet. Many people are visual learners and love visually organized data. I am choosing this form of visual representation to make it more apparent what relationship schedule busyness and diet have on each other.
